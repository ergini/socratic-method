# Interviews

The core ladder is in SKILL.md. These are the domain variants. Same eight functions, different surface. Adapt, do not recite.

**Conduct rules, in every domain:** one question at a time. React before you continue. Refuse hedges. Follow the surprise. Notice what they never mentioned. Do not rescue them from the pause. Read it back at the end and let them correct you.

## 1. Product / app / SaaS

1. In one word, what should someone feel while using this? *(Refuse two. Refuse "easy," "simple," and "clean," which are not feelings.)*
2. If a ruthless minimalist shipped v1, what is the one screen? What died?
3. What would you build into this that 95% of users would never notice, and the 5% who did would tell someone about?
4. Someone closes the app. What is the single thing left in their head?
5. What do people use instead of this today? *(If they say "nothing," they are wrong. People always have a workaround. Find it.)* What must yours be better at, and what are you happy to be worse at?
6. What would make this look amateur? What would make it look like a side project?
7. What will you refuse to do, even if it costs you users?
8. Who is this explicitly not for? *(If "everyone," they have not decided.)*

Follow-ups that earn their keep:
- "What is the moment the user decides this is worth paying for? Describe it."
- "What is the first thing that happens after signup, and why is that the first thing?"
- "What is in here because you want it, and not because they do?"

## 2. Design and brand

1. One word for the feeling. Then: what is the *opposite* of that word, and where has it crept in?
2. What would a minimalist delete? What survives, and why do you love it?
3. What would a craftsman add that nobody will consciously see? *(The invisible stitch: the letter-spacing, the empty state, the loading transition, the reply-to on the transactional email.)*
4. What must they remember? A colour, a shape, a phrase, a feeling. One thing.
5. Show me the closest thing that exists. What is it getting right that you are not? *(Push them. "Nothing" is a lie.)*
6. What would make this look cheap? What is the tell?
7. If you could only keep one: the logo, the typography, the colour, or the copy, which one is carrying the brand?
8. Who should look at this and think "not for me"?

## 3. Code and systems

1. In one word, what should a developer feel using this API or working in this codebase? *(Boring is an excellent answer.)*
2. What is the smallest version that would actually be useful? What did you cut, and are you sure it needs to come back?
3. What would a great engineer do here that a competent one would not bother with? *(The error message. The idempotency key. The migration rollback. The test for the case that "cannot happen.")*
4. Six months from now, what is the bug report this design produces? Write it now.
5. What is the boring, obvious solution? Why is it not good enough? *(Make them defend the interesting one.)*
6. What would make a senior engineer wince at this?
7. What are you refusing to do, even under pressure? *(No shared mutable state? No breaking the API? No cron jobs?)*
8. What is this system explicitly not going to handle? *(Scale? Multi-tenancy? Offline? Say it out loud so it stops being a silent assumption.)*

Follow-ups:
- "What breaks first at 100x?"
- "Is this a one-way door or a two-way door? You are treating it like the other one."
- "What is your evidence that this is the bottleneck, as opposed to your belief that it is?"

## 4. Writing

1. What should the reader feel at the end? One word.
2. If you deleted the first three paragraphs, would anything be lost? *(Usually not. This is the most reliable question in writing.)*
3. What is the one sentence you would fight to keep?
4. What is the one line they will still remember tomorrow? *(If there is no candidate, the piece has no point.)*
5. Who has written the closest thing to this, and better? What did they do that you did not?
6. What would make this read as AI-generated, or as a LinkedIn post? *(They know. Ask.)*
7. What are you not willing to say more simply, and why?
8. Who will hate this? *(If nobody, it says nothing.)*

## 5. Business, offer, and go-to-market

1. In one word, what does the buyer feel the moment they understand your offer?
2. What is the smallest thing you could sell tomorrow that someone would pay for?
3. What would you include that competitors would consider uneconomic? *(That is often the whole moat.)*
4. After the sales call ends, what is the one sentence they repeat to their colleague? *(Write it. That is your positioning.)*
5. Who do they buy from today? Why is that not embarrassing for them? *(If the incumbent is not embarrassing, "we're better" is not a strategy.)*
6. What would make you look like a fly-by-night operator?
7. What deal will you walk away from?
8. Which customer do you want to *lose*?

Follow-ups:
- "Write the launch announcement in one paragraph. If it is boring, why build it?"
- "What has to be true for this to be a big business? Do you believe those things, or do you need them to be true?"

## 6. The eight functions

Know what each rung is for, so you can improvise when the conversation goes somewhere better than your script.

| # | Function | The failure it catches |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **The emotional thesis** | No point of view. Everything downstream becomes arbitrary because there is no standard to judge against. |
| 2 | **Subtraction** | Bloat, and priorities that have never been forced to compete. |
| 3 | **The invisible stitch** | Commodity. The thing works and nobody loves it. |
| 4 | **The residue** | No memory, no word of mouth, no reason to return. |
| 5 | **Positioning and sacrifice** | A strategy that is really a wish. Being second-best at everything. |
| 6 | **The shame test** | Bad taste that they can feel but have not named. Fastest question in the set. |
| 7 | **The hill** | Drift. Without this, every compromise looks reasonable in isolation. **This is the line you will hold them to for the next year.** |
| 8 | **The knife** | Indecision dressed as ambition. |
